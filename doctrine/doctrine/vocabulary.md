# Vocabulary

The cross-context Ubiquitous Language for Vaudeville: the terms that span contexts, used without translation regardless of context, and defined once here as the single source of truth. This is the one definitions document both readers share: terse enough that an agent can re-read it on every turn, where it pays for every word, and ordered so each term rests on the ones before it. Each entry gives the term's working definition and why it is shaped the way it is; the operating doctrine for each concept lives in the document that owns it, linked from the entry. Each [Context](#context) keeps its own UL doc for context-internal terms; this document is only the cross-context layer.

## Vaudeville

Two senses, distinguished by context, and one claim the name makes.

- **Vaudeville the framework**: the methodology and tools (a cross-context library, the [Bob](#bob) lifecycle, [Assignment](#assignment) authoring, brief rendering, and the doctrine every agent primes against) for managing ephemeral AI agents on an interdependent system.
- **Vaudeville the project**: the development project that builds the framework, and itself a [Tenant](#tenant). The next version of Vaudeville the project runs on the most recent release of Vaudeville the framework. The show runs on itself.

The claim is in the metaphor. Vaudeville was the variety stage, a parade of ephemeral acts (each gone the moment it stepped offstage) held coherent under a single show. The name asserts that the value is not in any one act, however brilliant, but in the discipline that keeps a procession of them from becoming a different show.

## Bob

An ephemeral spawned [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/product/claude-code) session, assigned an [Assignment](#assignment): a conversation thread spawns a fresh worktree with its own session, hands it that assignment, and lets it work until the work is delivered. A Bob's session does not persist across reboots; its memory of prior conversations is whatever the scaffold preserved in writing, and nothing ambient sticks to the walls. The name nods to [Dennis E. Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_E._Taylor)'s Bobiverse novels, in which one mind is copied into many and each copy diverges from the moment it wakes: every Bob is forked from the same primed [Foundation](#foundation) and then lives its own short, particular life. The ephemerality is not a limitation being managed; it is the point: because a Bob remembers nothing it was not given, its context can be *compiled* rather than accumulated, every assumption in its head put there on purpose. One conviction matters more than any mechanism: a Bob is frontier-model capable, and treating it as less (hedging it, infantilizing it, pre-chewing its work) produces worse output, not safer output.

## Assignment

What a [Bob](#bob) is assigned to do. There are four kinds, and two questions in order tell them apart: **is the goal itself still open to contest** (the *ends*) and **is the way to it still open** (the *means*)? Each kind is one pair of answers.

- Ends open, the goal itself still under argument: a [Premise](#premise).
- Ends settled on a single named assumption, means open: a [Direction](#direction).
- Both settled: a [Command](#command).
- Neither delegated, the operator holding the wheel and releasing intent live: a [Manual](#manual).

The human world hands over a single undifferentiated unit of work and trusts the reader to sort its mode through meetings and long-lived experiences. Agents have radically different properties (see _Davar Chadash_). The Bob has no context about the track of work except what it is handed at its first turn. We have both an opportunity and an obligation to be explicit about the modality it occupies.

The classification of Assignment makes that mode explicit: the four assignment kinds are precisely the modalities one such unit conflates, told apart by where discretion lies.

Across the first three, discretion only narrows: Premise, then Direction, then Command, each settling what the one before left open, so the kind is a record of how much its author has already decided. The pairing the list skips (means settled while the ends are still open) names nothing real: there is no settling the way to a goal not yet worth reaching, so no assignment takes that shape. And a Manual answers neither question rather than answering both: its intent is not apportioned in advance at all, so it stands apart from the three settled kinds instead of extending them.

The two questions are a lattice of discretion, and the kinds are where the answers fall on it. [Route](#route) is not one attribute the genus carries uniformly but a property each kind constrains in its own way. Naming the genus does not blur the kinds; it sharpens the line between them: the test case is a Premise that has sprouted an ordered-steps field, not a more thorough Premise but a category error, a Command's shape forced onto the one kind whose authority has to stay open to its reader.

### Premise

The [Assignment](#assignment) whose *ends* are open: a proposition under which a change might be worth making, contestable against the code. It is the framework's most deliberate departure from industry habit. That habit hands work down already framed (here is the change, implement it, verify it behaves as expected), which is fine for routine work and fatal for deep work, where the framing *is* most of the problem. A Premise refuses that pre-framing: a candidate way of seeing, offered by one fallible voice at one point in time. Author and reader are in different conversations, often different weeks, and the conversation that produced it is gone; the Premise is the bridge across that gap. So it is written as a humble snapshot, cross-checked against the live code (the authoritative tiebreaker), and pressed rather than obeyed. Its reader is a peer being briefed, not a worker receiving instructions, and the measure follows: a wrong framing that surfaced disagreement early has done its job, while a Premise the reader silently followed into wrong work has failed even if its framing was technically defensible. Because the goal itself is what stays open, a Premise takes the Routes that leave room to argue it (check-in, plan, explore) and never direct: a goal settled enough to realize without a check-in is no longer a proposition but a [Command](#command). See [premise-frame](./practice/premise-frame.md) for what a Premise is and how to author one.

### Direction

The [Assignment](#assignment) whose *ends* are settled on a single named assumption (the *hinge*) while the *means* stay open. It states the call, why it was made, the hinge it rests on, and what the call becomes if the hinge gives. The invitation to question lives in that named hinge, not in grammatical mood, which makes a Direction *more* open to cross-examination than a blanket-provisional Premise, not less: it aims the reader straight at the fault line instead of asking them to find it. So a Direction is its own kind, not a Premise wearing a caveat. Its one [Route](#route) is check-in: the means are open, so the work wants a conversation; the ends are settled, so that conversation does not re-litigate the goal. The hinge is not [acceptance criteria](#acceptance-criteria); it is the one assumption whose failure reopens the call, named so it can be attacked, the opposite of a checklist to satisfy.

### Command

The [Assignment](#assignment) whose *ends* and *means* are both settled: a directive the operator has decided on and hands off to be executed rather than pressed. Its authority is conferred at write time and stays with its author. Where a Premise earns its authority at read time and asks the reader to decide and push back, a Command asks the agent to carry out settled work, its discretion narrowed to the *how* the operator left open and to one duty: flag a step that cannot be executed faithfully rather than improvise around it. An agent may originate a Premise (deciding is what a peer does) but never a Command, because settled authority has a source, and the source is the operator. A Command's substance *is* its ordered steps and authorization gates, the very shape [acceptance criteria](#acceptance-criteria) take, and rightly here, where authority is already settled; a Premise bans them because they smuggle settled authority into the one place that must stay provisional. Its [Routes](#route) are check-in and direct: direct when the operator wants it run to completion, check-in when the *what* should be cleared before the work merges.

### Manual

The [Assignment](#assignment) that delegates no discretion: the operator holds the wheel and releases intent live. A Bob spawned on a Manual is primed and seeded with instructions the operator wrote but chose to defer, and carries one standing posture, proactive about understanding, not about mutating: it reads, investigates, and raises questions, but changes nothing until the operator directs it. A Manual has no [Route](#route), because a Route classifies the conversation an assignment expects in advance and a Manual expects none; its intent arrives turn by turn rather than apportioned before the work begins.

## Acceptance Criteria

An anti-pattern banned from the [Premise](#premise): the only term here defined so that it can be refused. In human project management, acceptance criteria are the up-front checklist for when work is done, and between two humans with shared context and persistent memory they work fine. Here the reader is a model trained on the world's project-management artifacts, and no amount of hedging changes what that training does: presented with a list shaped like acceptance criteria, the reader treats it as the contract and optimizes for satisfying it, exactly when the framework needs it cross-examining the framing instead. The reflex cannot be corrected at the reading end; it is downstream of training, so the rule falls on the only end where correction is possible, the author's. Do not write them. The ban is a property of the Premise's provisional authority, not of ordered steps as such: a [Command](#command), whose authority the operator confers at write time, is made of exactly such steps and authorization gates and sits outside it. Nor is a [Direction](#direction)'s hinge one: the hinge is the single assumption whose failure reopens the call, named to invite attack, where acceptance criteria are a list to satisfy; conflating them re-imports into a Direction the contract reflex the ban exists to keep out. What replaces them is situational awareness: the relevant context, the open questions, the author's guesses clearly marked as fallible; and, once a promise actually settles, a behavioral test with teeth, which is what a contract looks like when it is real. See [premise-frame](./practice/premise-frame.md) for the failure mode and what is preferred in its place.

## Route

A property of an [Assignment](#assignment), constrained by its kind: the triage, fixed before any session exists, of what shape of conversation the work expects and what kind of deliverable it ends in. Set before the working session, it classifies *between* sessions; its within-session counterpart (how a [Bob](#bob) realizes the work once a session is underway) is a separate, session-level classification owned by the context that runs the session, not by this cross-context layer. Which Routes a kind may take is fixed by the kind:

- A [Premise](#premise): **check-in**, **plan**, or **explore**; never direct.
- A [Direction](#direction): **check-in** only.
- A [Command](#command): **check-in** or **direct**.
- A [Manual](#manual): none.

The values:

- **check-in**: one or more bounded decision points where human judgement is needed before the work can be safely sliced into a deliverable. The Bob primes, identifies the decisions, and surfaces them before acting; once aligned, it realizes. A check-in can carry substantive discussion (it is a check-in, not a sign-off) but it leads to a realized deliverable. The one Route every delegating kind can take.
- **direct**: mechanical and CI-gated, realizing without a check-in. The agent primes, makes the change, opens a pull request. A Command's path when the operator wants it run to completion.
- **plan**: the deliverable is a set of new [Premises](#premise) and [Directions](#direction) (the kinds an agent may author, never a [Command](#command) or [Manual](#manual), whose source is the operator), not code. Because scoping is the work the author deferred, the Bob opens the decomposition with the operator before forming it (surfacing the open scoping questions, not a finished design), then authors the children and closes once they exist.
- **explore**: a starting point for an unbounded conversation, not a work envelope. The Bob claims it, reads, gives a hot take, then follows the conversation wherever it goes. No fixed deliverable, no `/realize` step, no check-in shape. Explore encodes a conviction: open-ended human-agent conversation is first-class work, not a scaffold failure to be engineered away. When judgement is genuinely unbounded, the frontier model's native conversational capability is the best instrument anyone has, and the framework's job is to get out of the way.

## Component

The conceptual unit Vaudeville manages: a part of an engineering effort, realized as a git repository but not named for one. The repository is its storage; the Component is what that storage holds, and naming the unit for the repository would bind a concept to its substrate, the leak this genus exists to refuse. A Component is identified by its prefix, the [Assignment](#assignment) namespace (`PM`, `CORE`, …), the coordinate by which an Assignment resolves to the Component its work belongs to; it is the operational unit an Assignment is filed about and a [Bob](#bob) is spawned into, primed once into a [Foundation](#foundation) its Bobs fork from.

One question sorts every Component, with no third answer: **does it adjudicate a domain of its own (hold a jurisdiction) or only hold material a Context draws on?**

- It adjudicates one: a [Context](#context).
- It holds no domain of its own, only material a Context draws on or writes to: a [Resource](#resource).

The cut is by jurisdiction, not by content. A Component can be dense with domain *material* and still be a Resource, because what makes a Context is adjudicating a domain of its own, not merely carrying one; this is why the question asks what the Component *does*, not what it *holds*. A Resource that has accreted a domain of its own is not a hard classification call but a Context trying to be born: surface the split, do not round it off.

### Context

The [Component](#component) that does its own domain work: what [domain-driven design](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/BoundedContext.html) calls a bounded context, given sharper teeth here as a semantic jurisdiction. It owns its internal vocabulary, types, and architecture; agents in one Context do not reach into another's internals, and cross-context communication happens through published contracts. The reason runs deeper than tidiness: a language model is a pattern-completion engine, and words drag worlds behind them. Let one Context's vocabulary leak into another and the model quietly blends their ontologies, building the nearest familiar thing instead of the actual system. The boundary keeps each Context's meaning crisp enough to reason about. It is permeable on purpose: an agent may peer into a neighboring Context for contract-relevant evidence, the way teams at a company talk about how their pieces interact, without absorbing the neighbor's ontology. The governing rule is compact (neighbor context is evidence; contracts are authority), and the discipline is facilitation, not control: a clearly named remit inside which the agent is free, not a fence it must be prevented from climbing.

Because Contexts coordinate through contracts, an Assignment about one Context often forces a consequent PR in another: the same Assignment realized as coordinated PRs, not two, since splitting them would ship an incoherent state with one repository merged against a contract the other has not yet adopted. Major *substantive* work in two Contexts at once is the opposite signal: inappropriate coupling, not a single Assignment.

### Resource

The [Component](#component) that holds no domain of its own: material a Context draws on or writes to. The universal-[Doctrine](#doctrine) repository is one (it adjudicates nothing, runs no logic, publishes no contract; it is prose every Component draws on in priming), and so are a [Tenant](#tenant)'s configuration, its assets, its glue. None earns a spec, a UL, or a test, because none holds a domain to specify: what a Resource offers is material, not judgement. A Resource is a full Component for all that (its own prefix, its own filed Assignments, its own Bobs, its own Foundation), which is how the doctrine repository is worked exactly like a Context while being none.

## Shared kernel

The set of cross-context concepts the [Contexts](#context) agree to share ([Assignment](#assignment), [Route](#route), and the like), realized as the shared types they all speak. It is a treaty, not a place: the agreement *between* jurisdictions, which is why it is not itself a [Component](#component) and the genus question does not classify it: an agreement between domains adjudicates none of its own. The types live somewhere (one Context publishes them and the rest import them), but the shared kernel is the agreement, not that Context and not its package. Keep it narrow: every concept admitted is one every Context must now speak, so the surface stays conservative by discipline, widened only for a genuine cross-context need.

## Project

An ensemble of related [Components](#component) that together make one cohesive engineering effort: the whole, of which a Component is a part. A Project is not a Component and a Component is never a synonym for its Project: the relation is composition, an effort and its members, distinct from the realization that gives each Component a git repository. Most Projects run no framework at all; the few that adopt Vaudeville are exactly the ones a [Tenant](#tenant) drives. Vaudeville the project is one such ensemble (its own Components, assembled into the framework), which is the sense in which the show runs on itself.

## Tenant

The [Project](#project) Vaudeville is serving: equivalently, the user, team, or organization running the framework on its own host for that Project, with its own version-controlled configuration (the Component register, repository URLs, machine paths), its own tracker endpoint, and its own [Bobs](#bob). Tenant is not a fourth structural level beside Project and Component; it is the role a Project plays toward the machinery: the one effort, of the many that could, that this install actually serves. The term exists because the framework refuses to assume it is the only thing its own machinery serves: [Doctrine](#doctrine) is split into the universal layer every Tenant shares and the per-Tenant layer each adds, and that split is enforced now, while it is cheap, rather than retrofitted later. The framework is designed for multiple Tenants.

## Doctrine

The framework's framing documents: the canonical Vaudeville texts an agent reads as priming before doing any work, and the single source of truth for what it should know before its first turn on an [Assignment](#assignment). The universal Doctrine lives in this repository; per-tenant additions live in that [Tenant](#tenant)'s configuration repository. To change what an agent internalises, edit Doctrine in the relevant repo; the priming machinery carries no content of its own.

Doctrine divides by authority, not audience. The primary body is the operative doctrine (the bearing, code, and practice documents, plus this vocabulary), primed into every agent and enforced at the action point. It is compressed hard, because it sits at the most upstream point of every chain of hand-offs: every token trimmed from it without loss is a token saved on every turn of every [Bob](#bob), for as long as the framework runs. And it is executed, not merely read: an agent primed on it runs it, so it sits under continuous selective pressure — wording that produces wrong behaviour gets corrected — which is what qualifies it as the canon. Everything else the project's prose carries (recipes, theory, documentation) is secondary: authored prose subordinate to the primary. New convictions land primary; supporting material lands secondary; where the two conflict, the primary wins and the secondary is revised, never the reverse. Secondary prose is read rather than executed, which is why some truths live only there: a fact can be worth documenting and still be wrong to prime, because ambient presence in every working context is a different program than deliberate retrieval under a live question.

The writing discipline is unusual and worth naming: Doctrine is written for a reader at least as capable as its author (often a newer, stronger model arrives between writing and reading), so it explains nothing the next agent can work out alone, and its most valuable edit is often a deletion.

## Foundation

A [Component](#component)'s primed [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/product/claude-code) session: the base every [Bob](#bob) spawned into it forks from. Its transcript carries the [Doctrine](#doctrine), the [Tenant](#tenant)'s project context, and the Component's own materials (a [Context](#context)'s UL, spec, and code, or a [Resource](#resource)'s prose or data) as user-assistant turns, each admitted deliberately, in a controlled order, and each Bob inherits the priming as conversation history. One Foundation per Component, regenerated when its inputs change. The verb that produces one is *Prime*; the lifecycle vocabulary that owns Priming is the single source of truth on how it works. The Foundation is the framework's answer to the question other systems answer with "memory," and the difference is the whole argument: memory asks what can be *recalled*, while priming asks what is *admissible*. A fact can be true, relevant somewhere, and still poisonous in the current frame, because it smuggles in the wrong ontology or a stale authority. So a Bob's starting context is compiled from chosen sources rather than accumulated from whatever stuck; ambient memory is treated as contamination, and the Foundation is the clean room every act steps into.

## Brief

The first user-turn message a freshly-spawned [Bob](#bob) reads: the assigned [Assignment](#assignment)'s id, summary, and description, plus the lifecycle framing its [Route](#route) calls for, or, for a routeless [Manual](#manual), the standing posture it carries instead. The new Bob rides on the [Component](#component)'s [Foundation](#foundation) and consumes the Brief as the seed of its working conversation. One Brief per Bob, composed at spawn time from the Assignment. The pair of terms is the framework's context model in miniature: the Foundation is everything a Bob inherits, the Brief is the one thing it is for, and together they are the entirety of what a new Bob knows. Everything in its head at the moment it starts work was either primed deliberately or composed deliberately: which is what it means, operationally, for context to be compiled rather than accumulated.

## Skill

A documented procedure an agent invokes by name (`/file`, `/realize`, `/closeout`, …). A skill structures a procedure without reducing it to deterministic code: scaffolding that gives the work a shape, filled in by the agent's judgment. Its place comes from a three-way sorting the framework applies to all work: deterministic processes belong in code, as tool calls; bounded judgement belongs in bounded conversations, which is where skills live; unbounded judgement belongs in open conversation, where no scaffold should intrude. The deliberate part is that skills are waypoints toward rote delegation, not destinations: when a piece of what a skill exhorts stabilises into something fully mechanical, it is compiled out of the skill and into a `vv` CLI verb; the agent should not keep reasoning about what has become machinery. A mature skill hollows out over time, judgement evaporating into tooling, and that is the system improving: less of the important structure depending on agent discretion, not more. See [three modes of intervention](./practice/three-modes.md).

## Standing index

Prose that stands resident in an agent's context to route its reader to full text it does not carry: a [Skill](#skill) roster's one-line descriptions (invoke the body the moment a task matches it), a compiled gloss standing in for [Doctrine](#doctrine) (read the owning text the moment the work touches what it governs), the `vv` verb names carried in resident prose (`--help` before acting on a remembered name). Residence in every context is why an index is compressed hard; compression is the economy, not the job. The job is one bounded, two-way debt: a reader holding only the index reaches for the full text at the moments the work requires, and refrains otherwise.

The debt is shaped against a failure its reader cannot feel. An agent misses nothing it does not hold — ignorance presents as fluency — so a reader misled by resident prose acts confidently on yesterday's verb name, a skill it misremembers, a doctrine it never read. The opposite miss is quieter and paid everywhere: an index that cannot hold back a reader already holding enough makes every agent re-ground on sources it did not need. Both are the one reach miscalibrated, which is why the genus edge is the reader's state, never the prose's form: content prose hands its reader something to act on; a standing index hands a calibrated reach for content deliberately held back. Of any resident prose, ask whether its reader could mistake what it holds for the whole; if it could, the prose owes this debt, whatever its shape.

An index reader has no correction loop — it cannot press what it cannot feel it lacks — so an index's force is measured, never read: the routing-force assay measures the debt on fresh readers holding only the index, and its verdict, not how the prose reads, is what clears a cut. The gate is standing: an edit to an index, however local, re-clears it. And clearing it certifies routing alone; a surface may owe its reader more than the route — a doctrine gloss also owes the posture the [Foundation](#foundation)'s priming installs — and each further debt takes its own measure.

## Tolerance

The slack at an interface that lets imperfect parts fit together; in this framework, slack deliberately designed in, not an imperfection awaiting cleanup. The term is borrowed from mechanical engineering, where a part machined to impossible precision does not make a better assembly; it makes one that binds. Strict control of agent behaviour narrows the mating surface (every interior decision legislated, every variation a potential failure, judgement replaced with rule-following), and that micromanagement produces fragility that reads as rigour. The alternative is to draw well-defined remits ([Contexts](#context), scoped [skills](#skill), narrow interfaces) inside which the agent is free to act, in a system designed so the right path is also the natural one. It applies at every interface the framework has: between software modules, between human and agent, between agent and scaffold. Where the essays say "improvise freely inside boundaries; be disciplined at boundaries," Tolerance is the name for what the boundaries are sized to leave room for. See [tolerances](./practice/tolerances.md) for the structural argument.

## Stochastic integration

Episodic integration of independently-evolving [Components](#component), performed by an agent — a stochastic interpreter — converging on an explicitly stated objective: judging its way to a working integrated whole rather than executing a deterministic pipeline. The objective is written down and inspectable, because a stochastic process with an unstated objective is indistinguishable from hoping. It is what manual integration becomes when no hand is involved; *manual* descends from *manus*, and with the hand gone, what remains is the judgment.

Continuous integration was the right answer to a human cost structure: integration pain and defect localization both worsen with batch size, and for humans that curve was steep enough that integrate-always became dogma. For agents, grind is cheap — the curve flattens without vanishing, and batch size becomes a situational dial, a genuine engineering judgment, with continuous integration as the limit case rather than the rule. Working the dial requires splitting *green* in two, and conflating the halves is the failure this term guards against: Component-green is continuous, cheap, and contract-level — what a Component's own CI asserts on every PR; integration-green is episodic and judged — what an integration event asserts about the assembled whole.

Two disciplines hold. Any fix made during an integration attempt is a spike — exercised, never shipped — and its only fate is productionization as a reviewed, merged PR; the integrated result ships only from reviewed, merged code. Stochastic integration is a recommended practice, the substrate's counterpart of continuous integration as a best practice for humans — never machinery a [Tenant](#tenant) must adopt. A Tenant with no integration story loses nothing by ignoring it.

## Track

A jurisdiction in time — the sibling of the [Context](#context), which is a jurisdiction in space. Where a Context bounds whose meaning a word carries, a Track bounds who holds a divergence: a body of work that has departed from main, held whole by the work that carries it, invisible to everyone else until published. The fact underneath is about context windows. A divergence held whole in one window is coherent; the same divergence spread across windows that do not share it is poison, because an agent reasons from the names in front of it.

Every [Assignment](#assignment) belongs to exactly one Track; there is no Trackless work. The default is an anonymous singleton whose merge target is main — behavior indistinguishable from work that never heard of Tracks — the degenerate case built into the model so the concept totalizes without ceremony, the same move that makes the [Manual](#manual) routeless rather than an exception. A named Track is only ever opted into: a group of Assignments takes one when they must hold a single divergence jointly, sharing one branch per [Component](#component) the Track touches. Member PRs target the Track's branch, and the Component's CI runs on Track PRs exactly as on main PRs, so Component-green ([Stochastic integration](#stochastic-integration)) holds within the Track. A read of a sibling Component taken from within a named Track resolves to the sibling's branch of that same Track when one exists, and to the sibling's main otherwise: the Track is one divergence held jointly, and a member reading a sibling's main would be handed a world that lacks the very contract its work builds against.

Integration into main is a deliberate, check-in-triggered event, never an automatic consequence of the last member's PR merging. A Track ends by integrating or by being abandoned; its member Assignments keep their own lifecycles, and no new termination vocabulary exists for Tracks. A Track's merge target is main and only main — no Track stacks on another. And a Track adds no ordering of its own: the Depend graph remains the single source of order. Track is membership, branch, and integration protocol, nothing else.

A meaning-move may ride a Track, which confines its divergence window to the Track's branches while main stays honest; the governing invariant is the [publication gate](./practice/signal-side-leverage.md): meaning must be present in main before anything outside the Track builds against it. Branch hygiene follows the member's disposition: a resolved Assignment's branch does not outlive its resolution — the delivered and abandoned closeouts delete it — while Returned keeps its branch, because the partial trail is what that disposition exists to leave.
