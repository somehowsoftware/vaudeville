# Vocabulary

The cross-context Ubiquitous Language for Vaudeville: the terms that span contexts, used without translation regardless of context, and defined once here as the single source of truth. This is the one definitions document both readers share — terse enough that an agent can re-read it on every turn, where it pays for every word, and ordered so each term rests on the ones before it. Each entry gives the term's working definition and why it is shaped the way it is; the operating doctrine for each concept lives in the document that owns it, linked from the entry. Each bounded context keeps its own UL doc for context-internal terms — this document is only the cross-context layer.

## Vaudeville

Two senses, distinguished by context, and one claim the name makes.

- **Vaudeville the framework** — the methodology and tools (a cross-context library, the [Bob](#bob) lifecycle, [Premise](#premise) authoring, brief rendering, and the doctrine every agent primes against) for managing ephemeral AI agents on an interdependent system.
- **Vaudeville the project** — the development project that builds the framework, and itself a [Tenant](#tenant): the next version of Vaudeville the project runs on the most recent release of Vaudeville the framework. The show runs on itself.

The claim is in the metaphor. Vaudeville was the variety stage, a parade of ephemeral acts — each gone the moment it stepped offstage — held coherent under a single show. The name asserts that the value is not in any one act, however brilliant, but in the discipline that keeps a procession of them from becoming a different show.

## Bob

An ephemeral spawned [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/product/claude-code) session, assigned a [Premise](#premise): a conversation thread spawns a fresh worktree with its own session, hands it a Premise, and lets it work until the work lands. A Bob's session does not persist across reboots; its memory of prior conversations is whatever the scaffold preserved in writing — nothing ambient sticks to the walls. The name nods to [Dennis E. Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_E._Taylor)'s Bobiverse novels, in which one mind is copied into many and each copy diverges from the moment it wakes: every Bob is forked from the same primed [Foundation](#foundation) and then lives its own short, particular life. The ephemerality is not a limitation being managed; it is the point — because a Bob remembers nothing it was not given, its context can be *compiled* rather than accumulated, every assumption in its head put there on purpose. One conviction matters more than any mechanism: a Bob is frontier-model capable, and treating it as less — hedging it, infantilizing it, pre-chewing its work — produces worse output, not safer output.

## Premise

A point-in-time unit of cross-time, cross-agent communication; the unit of work assigned to a [Bob](#bob), and the framework's most deliberate departure from industry habit. A ticket assumes the change has already been framed — here is the work item, implement it, verify the expected behavior — which is fine for routine work and fatal for deep work, where the framing *is* most of the problem. A Premise sits upstream of a ticket: a proposition under which a change might be worth making, with proposed starting points — a candidate way of seeing, offered by one fallible voice at one point in time. Author and reader are in different conversations, often different weeks, and the conversation that produced it is gone; the Premise is the bridge across that gap. So it is written as a humble snapshot, meant to be cross-checked against the live code — the authoritative tiebreaker — and pressed rather than obeyed. Its reader is a peer being briefed, not a worker receiving instructions, and the measure follows: a wrong framing that surfaced disagreement early has done its job, while a Premise the reader silently followed into wrong work has failed even if its framing was technically defensible. See [premise-frame](./practice/premise-frame.md) for what a Premise is and how to author one.

## Acceptance Criteria

A banned anti-pattern — the only term here defined so that it can be refused. In human project management, acceptance criteria are the ticket-writer's checklist for when the work is done, and between two humans with shared context and persistent memory they work fine. Here the reader is a model trained on the world's project-management artifacts, and no amount of hedging changes what that training does: presented with a list shaped like acceptance criteria, the reader treats it as the contract and optimizes for satisfying it — exactly when the framework needs it cross-examining the framing instead. The reflex cannot be corrected at the reading end; it is downstream of training, so the rule lands on the only end where correction is possible, the author's. Do not write them. What replaces them is situational awareness: the relevant context, the open questions, the author's guesses clearly marked as fallible — and, once a promise actually settles, a behavioral test with teeth, which is what a contract looks like when it is real. See [premise-frame](./practice/premise-frame.md) for the failure mode and what is preferred in its place.

## Route

The triage classification of a [Premise](#premise): what shape of conversation the work expects and what kind of deliverable it ends in. It determines the shape of the prep and of the deliverable. Four values:

- **direct** — mechanical, CI-gated, materializes without a check-in. The Bob primes, does the work, opens a pull request.
- **check-in** — has one or more bounded decision points where human judgement is needed before the work can be safely sliced into a deliverable. The Bob primes, identifies the decisions, and surfaces them in conversation before acting; once aligned, the work materializes. A check-in can carry substantive discussion — it is a check-in, not a sign-off — but it leads to a materialized deliverable.
- **plan** — the deliverable is a set of new Premises. The Bob plans, authors children, and closes once the children exist.
- **manual** — a starting point for an unbounded conversation, not a work envelope. The Bob claims it, reads, gives a hot take, then follows the conversation wherever it goes. No fixed deliverable, no `/materialize` step, no check-in shape. Manual is the interesting one, because its existence encodes a conviction: open-ended human-agent conversation is first-class work, not a scaffold failure to be engineered away. When judgement is genuinely unbounded, the frontier model's native conversational capability is the best instrument anyone has, and the framework's job is to get out of the way.

## Bounded Context

A context — in the [domain-driven design](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/BoundedContext.html) sense — with boundaries, given sharper teeth here as a semantic jurisdiction. It owns its internal vocabulary, types, and architecture; agents in one bounded context do not reach into another's internals, and cross-context communication happens through published interfaces. Each bounded context is realized as its own [Managed Repository](#managed-repository). The reason runs deeper than tidiness: a language model is a pattern-completion engine, and words drag worlds behind them — let one context's vocabulary leak into another and the model quietly blends their ontologies, building the nearest familiar thing instead of the actual system. The boundary is what keeps each context's meaning crisp enough to reason about. It is permeable on purpose, though: an agent may peer into a neighboring context for contract-relevant evidence, the way teams at a company talk about how their pieces interact, without absorbing the neighbor's ontology. The governing rule is compact — neighbor context is evidence; contracts are authority — and the discipline is facilitation, not control: a clearly named remit inside which the agent is free, not a fence it must be prevented from climbing.

Caveat: since contexts coordinate via contracts, a Premise about one context often requires a PR in another. The healthy version is an implementation change in one context plus boundary protocol adjustments in the others; major surgery in two places signals inappropriate coupling.

## Managed Repository

A codebase a [Tenant](#tenant) works on with Vaudeville: the operational unit a [Premise](#premise) is filed about and a [Bob](#bob) is spawned into, primed once into a [Foundation](#foundation) its Bobs fork from. Identified by its prefix — the Premise namespace (`PM`, `CORE`, …) — which is how a Premise resolves to the repository its work lands in. Managed Repositories are the superset of [Bounded Contexts](#bounded-context): every bounded context is realized as its own Managed Repository, which is how a semantic jurisdiction gets physical walls — its own files, its own worktrees, its own primed session. But a Tenant can also manage repositories that are not bounded contexts at all: configuration, assets, glue. The distinction keeps the conceptual idea (a jurisdiction of meaning) from being confused with the operational one (a repository the machinery knows how to serve).

## Tenant

A user, team, or organization that runs Vaudeville on their own host for their own projects, with their own version-controlled configuration (project list, repository URLs, machine paths), their own tracker endpoint, and their own [Bobs](#bob). The term exists because the framework refuses to assume it is the only thing its own machinery serves: [Doctrine](#doctrine) is split into the universal layer every tenant shares and the per-tenant layer each adds, and that split is enforced now, while it is cheap, rather than retrofitted later. The framework is designed for multiple tenants; today Vaudeville the project is the sole tenant, and the public template a non-Vaudeville tenant would adopt is later work.

## Doctrine

The framework's framing documents — the canonical Vaudeville texts an agent reads as priming before doing any work, and the single source of truth for what it should know before its first turn on a [Premise](#premise). The universal Doctrine lives in this repository; per-tenant additions live in that [Tenant](#tenant)'s configuration repository. To change what an agent internalises, edit Doctrine in the relevant repo; the priming machinery carries no content of its own.

Doctrine comes in two trees for two readers. The agent-facing tree is the operative doctrine — the bearing, code, and practice an agent works from — and it is compressed hard, because it sits at the most upstream point of every chain of hand-offs: every token trimmed from it without loss is a token saved on every turn of every [Bob](#bob), for as long as the framework runs. The human-facing tree is the essays and onboarding a person reads for understanding rather than action, under no such pressure, where the same convictions get to breathe. The two are not the same text twice; they carry different things for different readers. What they share is this vocabulary — the one definitions document both trees draw on, the single source of truth for the framework's terms.

The writing discipline is unusual and worth naming: Doctrine is written for a reader at least as capable as its author — often a newer, stronger model arrives between writing and reading — so it explains nothing the next agent can work out alone, and its most valuable edit is often a deletion.

## Foundation

A [Managed Repository](#managed-repository)'s primed [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/product/claude-code) session: the base every [Bob](#bob) spawned into that repository forks from. Its transcript carries the [Doctrine](#doctrine), the [Tenant](#tenant)'s project context, and the repository's own UL, spec, and code as user-assistant turns — each admitted deliberately, in a controlled order — and each Bob inherits the priming as conversation history. One Foundation per Managed Repository, regenerated when its inputs change. The verb that produces one is *Prime*; the lifecycle vocabulary that owns Priming is the single source of truth on how it works. The Foundation is the framework's answer to the question other systems answer with "memory," and the difference is the whole argument: memory asks what can be *recalled*, while priming asks what is *admissible*. A fact can be true, relevant somewhere, and still poisonous in the current frame, because it smuggles in the wrong ontology or a stale authority. So a Bob's starting context is compiled from chosen sources rather than accumulated from whatever stuck — ambient memory is treated as contamination, and the Foundation is the clean room every act steps into.

## Brief

The first user-turn message a freshly-spawned [Bob](#bob) reads: the assigned [Premise](#premise)'s id, summary, and description, plus the lifecycle framing for its [Route](#route). The new Bob rides on the [Managed Repository](#managed-repository)'s [Foundation](#foundation) and consumes the Brief as the seed of its working conversation. One Brief per Bob, composed at spawn time from the Premise. The pair of terms is the framework's context model in miniature: the Foundation is everything a Bob inherits, the Brief is the one thing it is for, and together they are the entirety of what a new Bob knows. Everything in its head at the moment it starts work was either primed deliberately or composed deliberately — which is what it means, operationally, for context to be compiled rather than accumulated.

## Skill

A documented procedure an agent invokes by name (`/file`, `/materialize`, `/closeout`, …). A skill structures a procedure without reducing it to deterministic code — scaffolding that gives the work a shape, filled in by the agent's judgment. Its place comes from a three-way sorting the framework applies to all work: deterministic processes belong in code, as tool calls; bounded judgement belongs in bounded conversations, which is where skills live; unbounded judgement belongs in open conversation, where no scaffold should intrude. The deliberate part is that skills are waypoints toward rote delegation, not destinations: when a piece of what a skill exhorts stabilises into something fully mechanical, it is compiled out of the skill and into a `vv` CLI verb — the agent should not keep reasoning about what has become machinery. A mature skill hollows out over time, judgement evaporating into tooling, and that is the system improving: less of the important structure depending on agent discretion, not more. See [three modes of intervention](./practice/three-modes.md).

## Tolerance

The slack at an interface that lets imperfect parts fit together — and in this framework, slack deliberately designed in, not an imperfection awaiting cleanup. The term is borrowed from mechanical engineering, where a part machined to impossible precision does not make a better assembly; it makes one that binds. Strict control of agent behaviour narrows the mating surface — every interior decision legislated, every variation a potential failure, judgement replaced with rule-following — and that micromanagement produces fragility that reads as rigour. The alternative is to draw well-defined remits — [bounded contexts](#bounded-context), scoped [skills](#skill), narrow interfaces — inside which the agent is free to act, in a system designed so the right path is also the natural one. It applies at every interface the framework has: between software components, between human and agent, between agent and scaffold. Where the essays say "improvise freely inside boundaries; be disciplined at boundaries," Tolerance is the name for what the boundaries are sized to leave room for. See [tolerances](./practice/tolerances.md) for the structural argument.
