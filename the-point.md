# The point

## A show of ephemeral acts

Vaudeville was the variety form in which a parade of ephemeral acts (a juggler, a singer, a comedian, each gone the moment it stepped offstage) shared one stage under a single coherent show. That is the shape of this framework, and the reason for the name. A [Bob](doctrine/vocabulary.md#bob) is an ephemeral act: a fresh [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/product/claude-code) session spawned into a worktree, handed one [Assignment](doctrine/vocabulary.md#assignment), run until the work is delivered, then gone. The operator is the show-runner, the one who keeps a variety of independent acts coherent under a single direction.

That is the shape. What the show is *for* takes longer to say, and it is the subject of this essay. But its one indispensable sentence can be said immediately: dialectical engineering is not about coding. The point is to produce work products that transcend what either the human or the agent could have produced alone, and the coding is largely a natural consequence of the preceding steps having been taken with earnestness, commitment, and focus. The disciplines this essay dwells on (rigorous coding standards, domain modeling, coherence maintained across sessions) are major ingredients, and neglecting any of them sinks the whole. But they are no more what the output is than a cake is flour and eggs. 

## Velocity, reach, depth

There are basically three things you can try to get out of agentic coding: velocity, reach, and depth.

Velocity means building what you already understand, very fast. Reach means building what you do not personally know how to build but somebody does. The knowledge exists in the model's learned distribution, and the agent carries you across the gap: a parser, a sync protocol, a stack you have never touched. Nearly every tool in this space is aimed at one of those two, for the sound commercial reason that nearly all software needs nothing else.

Depth is the third thing, and it is almost never the goal. Depth means building software out of tools you already know, at a level of sophistication you struggle to attain: systems whose parts are individually intelligible, but whose total coordination burden exceeds what one mind can hold in stable relation. "Out of reach" is not quite the right phrase; out of *stable unaided synthesis* is. You can glimpse the whole system. You can reason about any local piece. You may even understand the entire thing, intermittently. What you cannot do is keep every distinction alive, correctly weighted, and uncorrupted long enough to implement the thing without collapse, simplification, or quiet damage. Depth is where the opening claim stops being a slogan: the work product genuinely exceeds what either party could produce alone. The human exceeds it for the reasons just given. The agent exceeds it because no training distribution hands it this system's ontology, and left to itself it will substitute the nearest familiar one.

The project that produced this framework is a fair specimen of the class: a task engine whose predicate algebra is a lossless superset of both [OmniFocus](https://www.omnigroup.com/omnifocus/)'s and [TaskWarrior](https://taskwarrior.org/)'s semantics, over a two-level dependency graph, with [Lamport-clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamport_timestamp)-based sync. The domain looks deceptively ordinary (everyone understands tasks, projects, due dates) and the familiarity is the trap. "Available," "blocked," "deferred," "waiting," "next action": some of these concepts overlap in name but not in behavior, some in behavior but not in name; some are stored facts, some are derivations, some are user-interface affordances masquerading as domain primitives. Underneath them, causal ordering is not conflict resolution, conflict resolution is not user intent, and a recurrence rule edited concurrently on two devices is not the same kind of conflict as a title edited twice. That is five-dimensional chess. It simply does not fit in a brain. But it fits in a context window, if the context can be kept focused enough.

That last clause is the entire framework, stated as a condition. And it deserves an immediate sobering: context *size* is not the asset; controlled *representation* is the asset. The system does not fit in the brain as an intuition, and it will not fit in a context window as a heap of prose either. It fits as a disciplined vocabulary, explicit contracts, and executable invariants that a frontier model can traverse without anyone needing to hold the whole structure intuitively at once.

There is a whole category of software that does not exist because depth is, by definition, out of one's reach. Historically the frontier of software sophistication was bounded by how well you could coordinate the very brightest engineers, and that coordination is not economical outside large research operations. Frontier models change the cost structure: serious local engineering capability is now available right out of the box, and the engineers who *were* allocated to frontier problems can reach even further than before. What does not come out of the box is stability. The problem becomes scaling depth, and that is a coherence problem, a control problem. Hence the terse formulation the operative doctrine carries: Vaudeville is for software that exceeds the grasp of a single human intelligence, where the dominant cost is not writing the code but holding a coherent picture of a system under constant change, and keeping every contributor's actions aligned with that picture. Most people writing software with agents do not have this problem and need none of this. Vaudeville is for when you do.

## How depth fails

A weak model fails obviously. A strong model fails plausibly.

The failure that matters in depth work is not bad code; coding agents stopped writing obviously bad code a while ago. The failure that matters is plausible code that quietly changes the meaning of the system. A predicate becomes a filter; a filter becomes a state transition; the dependency graph leaks into scheduling semantics; a Lamport clock gets treated as a merge policy; a user-facing metaphor becomes a domain primitive; the test suite validates examples while the algebra underneath them dies. Every local move looks reasonable. The system is now wrong globally, and it keeps working right up until it has to compose.

The mechanism is not stupidity; it is suggestibility. A language model is a pattern-completion engine, and names carry implementation gravity: say "project," "next action," "ticket," or "review," and the model does not read a label, it inhales a whole cloud of learned associations and begins, helpfully, to build the nearest familiar thing. Sometimes the fix is as simple as renaming a concept that has human-world connotations so the agent does not fall into cosplaying that pattern. This is not pedantry; it is prior management. Names are control surfaces, and a misleading name is an architectural bug.

The same logic governs every hand-off in the system, because the rate-limiting failure mode of the whole enterprise is information corruption across boundaries: specs into Assignments, Assignments into work, work into code, conversation into summary. Each transmission compresses, and each compression can drop a distinction, import a false implication, or convert a living idea into a role-play object. This sounds like a memory problem and is in fact adjacent to one. It has much more to do with very human practices like mindfulness, organization, and humility than with anything technical. Memory asks, "can we retrieve the prior thing?" The real question is, "did the thing survive translation without losing its purpose?" Do not maximize tokens. Do not maximize agents. Maximize preserved insight per boundary crossing.

Put together: when frontier models make local work cheap, global coherence becomes the bottleneck. The characteristic product of an undisciplined agentic process at depth is elaborate wrongness: a large amount of competent-looking work organized around a slightly corrupted premise.

## The engine: targeted friction

It would be natural to stop there and conclude that this framework is a preservation discipline: [Contexts](doctrine/vocabulary.md#context), contracts, tests, careful hand-offs, all in the service of keeping meaning intact. That conclusion is incomplete in a way that misses the point. Coherence is the containment layer, not the engine. A perfectly coherent system can preserve a mediocre ontology; it can defend an early mistake too well; it can become a museum of its first decent abstraction. The engine is friction.

The system is optimized to force every reasoning-capable mind in it (operator and agents alike) to struggle with the fundamental questions, from the highest altitude down:

Is this even the right kind of thing to want? Is this the right way of looking at it? Is this the right way of approaching it? Where does the current approach make the next move harder? How do we prove we are meeting our promises? And only then: how do we meet them?

We don't go down a checklist. We struggle, long-form, and we keep the distractions out by delegating them away. Most engineering process starts at the bottom of that ladder, treating "how do we build it?" as the main question. For depth work the order inverts: by the time the upper questions have been forced into clarity, the actual coding is the least interesting problem, trivially left to existing tools that are genuinely good at it. When the process is working, the coding happens (and happens well) largely as a natural consequence of those earlier steps having been taken with earnestness, commitment, and focus. Implementation is not the heroic act; it is the residue the dialectic leaves behind when it is done. This is not because code is easy in general. It is because in this class of work the bottleneck is preventing the wrong abstraction from becoming structural, and code is where wrong abstractions go to harden into the foundation.

The operating rule has two halves that only work together: maximize friction against the unresolved conceptual problem, and minimize friction everywhere else. Make the central question hard to evade and everything peripheral easy to delegate. This requires telling two kinds of friction apart. Dialectical friction sharpens the concept: it forces distinctions, reveals contradictions, prevents premature closure. Procedural friction consumes attention: artifacts produced because the workflow demands artifacts, compliance mistaken for rigor. A lot of scaffolds add steps and call the result discipline. The largest standing risk of a framework like this one is building a bureaucracy for agents, and the defense is the ruthless discrimination between those two frictions. That discrimination includes noticing when it actually *is* the right time to get out of the way and let the agent vibe like the other systems do. Vibes are not rejected here; they are contained. Improvise freely inside boundaries; be disciplined at boundaries. Looseness is fine anywhere it cannot define the ontology.

What the friction targets, concretely, is escape. Premature implementation is usually avoidance: code is concrete and therefore emotionally relieving (you can run tests, open a pull request, feel progress) while a wrong premise gets buried deeper under competent work. The machine is built to make that escape difficult for everyone in it. The agent is not allowed to glide past the central uncertainty by producing plausible code. The operator is not allowed to glide past it by accepting a familiar label. The tests are not allowed to certify happy paths while the algebra rots.

## Two unreliable parties

Why dialectic, specifically? Because of who is at the table.

The human is unreliable. The agent is unreliable. This is not a flaw in the design; it *is* the design. The two fail in different ways, and the only known instrument for catching what one of them has missed is opposable conversation with the other. That is where the first word of *dialectical engineering* comes from, and it is why the agent is asked to carry a spine. A fluent, agreeable concession costs nothing and surfaces nothing; honest opposition (naming the bad thing bad, holding a position until the argument against it actually wins) is the one thing in the exchange the operator could not have produced alone. An agent that only agrees has quietly removed itself from the work.

The deeper reason the dialectic is central is this: nobody at the table has the answers, almost by definition. If the operator had them, none of this would be necessary: write tickets, issue prompts, review diffs. Dialectical engineering begins where neither party holds the answer in usable form. The human brings taste, stakes, suspicion, and the ability to feel that a frame is wrong before being able to say why. The agent brings stamina, breadth, formal manipulation, and the patience to grind through implications. The artifacts bring memory and resistance; the tests have teeth. The answer is produced by the relation, under constraint. The agent and the operator enable one another to create things neither could create alone. That is dialectical engineering, and Vaudeville is one instantiation of it: mostly drawn from existing engineering and management disciplines, iteratively refined for the alien-ness of agents.

This is also why the characteristic unit of work is a Premise and not a ticket. A ticket assumes the change has already been framed: here is the work item, implement it, verify the expected behavior. That is fine for velocity and fatal for depth, where the framing *is* most of the problem. A Premise refuses that pre-framing: a proposition under which a change might be worth making, with proposed relevant starting points, a candidate way of seeing offered by one fallible voice at one point in time, meant to be cross-checked against the live code and pressed rather than obeyed. (It is one of four kinds of assignment the framework separates out, told apart by where the operator has left discretion; the Premise is the kind for work whose goal is still in contention, and the one depth lives in. [Concepts](./concepts.md) draws the full distinction.) A wrong framing that surfaced disagreement early has done its job; a Premise the reader silently followed into wrong work has failed even if its framing was technically defensible. The Premise bans [acceptance criteria](doctrine/vocabulary.md#acceptance-criteria) outright, because a reader trained on the world's project-management artifacts cannot help treating them as the contract no matter how they are hedged, and the only end of that channel where the reflex can be corrected is the author's. The ban is the Premise's own, not a rule against ordered steps as such, which a Command is rightly built from.

## What the struggle must leave behind

Friction is not virtue. A process that worships difficulty will select for minds that are good at enduring process rather than finding truth, and long-form struggle that produces only more prose has failed. The standard is residue: thought that leaves something essential behind. A renamed primitive that stops the model from importing the wrong world. A Context split in two where one jurisdiction was secretly hosting a second. A rejected interpretation, written down, that guards the next reader against the same seduction. A promise converted from prose into a behavioral test with teeth. A repeated judgment promoted out of the agentic loop entirely.

That last item is its own principle, and it may be the most practical sentence in this essay: the agent should not keep reasoning about things that have become mechanical. When there is enough of a pattern to something that keeps happening, it gets pulled out of the agentic loop and into a deterministic layer (a tool, a schema, a check, a CLI verb) so the agent stays focused on its comparative advantage. This is why the framework's [skills](doctrine/vocabulary.md#skill) are explicitly waypoints toward rote delegation: a skill structures a procedure that still needs judgment, and parts of it crystallize into tool calls as their deterministic shape becomes clear. The agent's intelligence is the scarce resource; do not spend it on what a tool can do. This also yields a clean criterion of progress. The system is not improving because agents complete more tasks; it improves when less of the important structure depends on agent discretion. A mature instance becomes *more* deterministic over time, not less. The agent remains central, but its centrality moves upward, from re-solving known transformations to noticing which transformations have stabilized enough to compile.

The same accounting governs the code itself. The measure of any change is not how many lines it adds or how much coverage it claims; it is whether the next person to work here will have an easier time than the last: whether a bug in this corner would announce itself or hide. The framing is surplus, not debt: every change is a deposit on a compounding cycle or a withdrawal from it, and the whole body of code discipline (correctness over expedience, names that hit you over the head, contracts written as tests before the code that satisfies them) exists to keep that cycle compounding. "Done" does not mean the tests pass. Done means the system is better positioned for the next Assignment than it was for this one. All of it is ingredient discipline: neglect it and the ceiling drops quietly, from work that transcends its makers to work that merely accumulates their effort.

And the operator's role, through all of this, is neither prompt-writer nor line-by-line reviewer. The operator is the adjudicator of meaning: choosing names, accepting and rejecting Premises, deciding which distinctions matter, approving the promotion of insight into machinery, and noticing when the process has slipped from friction into ceremony. Which is, after all, what the show-runner of a variety stage actually does: not perform the acts, but keep a parade of them from becoming a different show.

## Stronger models make this more valuable, not less

The conversation this essay distills began as anxiety. A new model ships above the previous frontier (this happens every few months) and the chorus arrives on schedule: surely scaffolds like this one are about to become superfluous; surely the model will just internalize all of it. Examined closely, the anxiety inverts.

Capability growth moves value from compensation scaffolds to governance scaffolds. Anything in a scaffold that merely patches model weakness (boilerplate prompting, brittle step lists, context tricks for a model that loses track of its task) decays as models improve, and deserves to; expect those layers to be shed and welcome the shedding. But the governing questions do not decay, because they were never weaknesses of the model. What should we want? What frame? What is admissible into this context? What has stabilized enough to become machinery? What must not be allowed to blur? These are properties of the relationship between local capability and global meaning, and a stronger model sharpens the stakes on every one of them: it fails more plausibly, carries a wrong abstraction farther before anyone notices, and produces coherent-looking wrong systems faster and at larger scale.

There is also a second-order effect running the same direction: demanding scaffolds become more useful as models strengthen, because the model can finally sustain them. A weak model collapses under a high-friction dialectical process; it treats critique, revision, and adversarial decomposition as noise and reverts to generic coding behavior. A strong model can hold a position, attack a premise, notice an implication four layers down, and participate in designing the proof of a promise. Rising capability did not make this system less relevant; it moved it from interesting-but-model-limited to operational.

So the durable claim is this: as models improve, the scarce resource shifts from code generation to conceptual governance. Better agents make more things possible. They also make wrong things possible at greater speed and depth. Vaudeville is aimed at the layer that decides which is which.

## Same parts, different failure modes

Show this framework to almost anyone in the field and the response boils down to "you and everyone else." It has agents, worktrees, skills, tests, pull requests, review loops, a memory story. So does every other harness on the shelf. The parts list proves nothing, and arguing from the parts list is a category error.

[Knipex](https://www.knipex.com/) makes a pliers. [Harbor Freight](https://www.harborfreight.com/) makes a pliers. They are not the same tool. Both grip, cut, pivot, and fit in a toolbox; the difference appears under load: jaw alignment, hardening, leverage geometry, and what happens to the workpiece when force is applied. Most scaffolds are built to get more force out of the agent. This one is built so the workpiece is not marred while force is applied. The workpiece is the conceptual structure of the system, and the load case is an agent doing a large amount of locally plausible work.

So the honest differentiation runs by failure mode, not by component. This is not a memory system: memory asks what can be recalled, while this work asks what is *admissible*. A fact can be true, relevant somewhere, and still poisonous in the current frame, because it smuggles in the wrong ontology or a stale authority. That is why ambient memory is treated here as contamination, and why a Bob's context is compiled rather than accumulated: a [Foundation](doctrine/vocabulary.md#foundation) primed deliberately from doctrine and the repository's own materials, a [Brief](doctrine/vocabulary.md#brief) composed from the Assignment, and nothing ambient sticking to the walls. This is not a workflow: a workflow says which steps happen, while the question here is which meanings survive the steps. You can have a beautiful workflow that corrupts the domain model at every crossing. This is not multi-agent debate: two agents arguing from the wrong frame converge on a more polished wrong answer, and token expenditure is search, not coherence. And it is not prompt engineering: the deeper issue is representation, and the more important a concept is, the less it should depend on the model remembering it as prose.

Humility is owed here, and given. The serious end of the industry is circling nearby problems under names like context engineering, harness engineering, and spec-driven development, and the pieces of this framework have deep ancestry: bounded contexts, [anti-corruption layers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain-driven_design), behavioral tests, deterministic checks did not appear here first. The distinction is the optimization target. Most harnesses ask: how can an agent complete more software tasks with less human intervention? This one asks: how can a human-agent system preserve, pressure, and deepen a conceptual structure whose coordination burden is too high for ordinary implementation? In full, the defensible claim reads: there is a class of software that is uneconomical or cognitively unstable for individuals and small teams because the semantic coordination burden is too high; frontier models make that class accessible, but only with machinery that protects conceptual coherence while applying pressure to it; Vaudeville is one attempt at that machinery; and the attempt may fail while the problem remains exactly as described: unusual, valuable, and growing more valuable with every increase in model capability.

## Why the doctrine reads the way it does

The doctrine is written for a reader at least as capable as its author, because that reader genuinely is. A newer, stronger model often arrives between the writing of a doctrine and its reading. So it does not explain what the next agent can work out on its own; it communicates only the slice of context the next agent could not have, because that context was not in the conversation that produced the work. Authoring doctrine is the act of finding that slice, which makes it, deliberately, a discipline of omission: the most valuable edit is often a deletion.

It is also why the agent-facing tree is compressed so hard. The rate-limiting failure in all of this is information corruption across a chain of lossy hand-offs, and a fix at the producer end of that chain multiplies across every reader downstream, while a fix at the consumer end helps only once. The doctrine is the most upstream artifact there is. Every token trimmed from it without loss is a token saved on every turn of every Bob, for as long as the framework runs. That leverage is what the split between this tree and the agent-facing one was built to capture.

## How to read this

The convictions in this essay did not come from software engineering, mostly; they were dragged in from fields that rarely get within shouting distance of a build pipeline. [Weaponized philosophy](./weaponized-philosophy.md) names those sources and shows the rent each one pays in machinery. The law itself lives in [`doctrine/`](doctrine/). Read this side for understanding, that side for the rules. And when you grow this human canon, let it stay what it is: the place where the convictions are allowed to breathe, so that the operative tree can stay lean.

And if a single sentence is to survive the reading, let it be the first one: dialectical engineering is not about coding. Everything catalogued here (the contracts, the friction, the compiled context, the tests with teeth) is the flour and the eggs, indispensable and insufficient. The cake is the work neither mind could have made alone.
