# Best Practices Are Answers

Every canonical engineering practice is the conclusion of an argument. The conclusion survives in the culture as a rule; the premises quietly fall away. This essay recovers those premises for one family of rules and works through what happens when the central one — the cost of human labor at a particular kind of task — stops holding.

## The question continuous integration answered

Fowler's founding argument for continuous integration is a practitioner essay, not a study — and unusually candid about what the practice is for. The fundamental benefit, he writes, is escaping "integration hell": the stretches a team spends hunting bugs created when one person's work steps on another's. Integration effort grows more than linearly with the time between integrations, so the fix is to integrate constantly, in the smallest batches automation permits (Fowler, martinfowler.com, 2000). Every load-bearing term in that argument is human: people hunting interaction bugs, inferring what a colleague meant, losing the thread as divergence accumulates.

The scholarly corroboration is about humans too. Merge conflicts — the moments when two divergent lines of work must be reconciled — are inevitable under parallel work, disruptive to those who hit them, and measurably damaging to team productivity and morale (McKee et al.; Nelson et al.; Brindescu et al.). Google's argument for the monorepo — one repository as the common source of truth for tens of thousands of developers — is a coordination-at-scale argument (Potvin & Levenberg, CACM, 2016); the monorepo evidence base beyond it is mostly company engineering reports — grey literature, load-bearing, flagged as such.

None of this is a debunking. These were correct answers — to a question about humans: given that reconciling divergent work burns scarce human attention, and burns more than linearly as divergence grows, how should work be batched? Change the given and the answer comes up for re-derivation — not for reversal by default.

## How an answer becomes a truth

The move from answer to truth has well-studied mechanics. Standards and classifications become the scaffolding of information infrastructure: they organize action, sink into the background, and stop being seen as chosen at all (Bowker & Star, *Sorting Things Out*, MIT Press, 1999; Star & Ruhleder, ISR, 1996). A team does not experience its pipeline, branch protections, and review gates as positions once argued from premises, but as the environment work passes through. Infrastructure becomes visible only when it breaks — so a practice's premises get re-examined only in a crisis, exactly when re-examination is hardest.

Fowler watched this happen to his own field and named it: the fight, he said, is against "the Agile Industrial Complex and its habit of imposing process upon teams" — the author of the practice diagnosing its dogmatization. And the dogma is measurably hollow at the edges: organizations ratchet away from canonical agile practice step by step while keeping the label (Berente et al., ISR), and the tailoring literature finds practice selection driven by local context, with no universal recipe on offer (Campanelli & Parreiras, JSS, 2015). Whatever "best practice" means, it is not a description of what the organizations invoking it do.

## What the evidence under the dogma shows

Press on the canon and it gives more than its confidence suggests. Test-driven development, in the studies with the highest rigor and relevance, improves external quality at a cost in productivity (Munir et al., IST, 2014); the body of evidence overall is "contradictory and inconclusive" (Ghafari et al., ESEM, 2020); and the treatment itself is scarcer than the discourse — in one large observational study, projects claiming TDD actually followed it in 12% of cases (Karac & Turhan, IEEE Software, 2018): the label names an identity more often than a treatment. Pair programming's meta-analysis finds a small quality effect bought with more total effort, plus signs of publication bias (Hannay et al., IST, 2009).

Continuous integration's own evidence has quietly reframed its benefit. Across 162,653 pull requests in 87 projects, over half of projects saw *longer* merged-PR lifetimes after adopting CI; the durable benefit, the authors conclude, is confidence and quality, not delivery speed (Bernardo et al., MSR, 2018). The practice is real; the folklore — "CI makes teams faster" — is not what the measurements say. What survives measurement is a cost-structure claim: CI reliably buys validation and confidence against a specific human failure mode. That is humbler than "best practice," and more useful: it says exactly what any successor must preserve.

## The honest split

The same literature that historicizes integration cadence *defends* other practices outright.

Code review converges: radically different projects arrive independently at the same lightweight practice (Rigby & Bird, ESEC/FSE, 2013). Its links to quality are robust — review coverage and participation predict fewer post-release defects (McIntosh & Hassan, EMSE, 2016) — and its actual value is substantially knowledge transfer and coordination, not just defect-finding (Bacchelli & Bird, ICSE, 2013). Review answers a question any substrate faces: does a second reader catch what the author cannot see, and does what the author knows spread? Tests answer another: what does this unit promise, stated so no reader can charitably misread it — a need that grows stronger, not weaker, when the reader completes patterns.

So this is not a claim that all practice is relative. Some practices are portable across substrates because the question they answer travels; others are welded to the cost structure that asked. The framework's [map](../map.md) sorts every re-derivation into three landing zones — the familiar answer, the abandoned answer, the unprecedented answer — and the literature supports exactly that split: tests and review re-derive to the familiar answer; integration cadence does not.

## The perennial discipline, newly enforceable

There has long been a body of practice for the work where coherence is the binding constraint: pure functions, immutable data, names drawn from a ubiquitous language, the domain modeled before mechanism, effects pushed to the edges, types that make illegal states unrepresentable. It belongs to no single school — it takes bounded contexts and the ubiquitous language from Evans, tests-as-design and tidy-first from Beck, the functional synthesis from Wlaschin, simple-versus-easy from Hickey, functional-core/imperative-shell from Bernhardt, with roots running back through Lisp, ML, Erlang, and the Unix tradition, and an anomalous debt to Larry Wall's linguist instinct that names are the primary design surface and a codebase is a language a team speaks. No label captures it because it is a trade tradition rather than a methodology: passed by example, assembled by sustained individual taste, never quite the same twice — which is exactly the property that kept it from ever surviving mainstream engineering economics.

That failure to take root was never the mainstream's failure. Mainstream practice is correctly optimized for replaceable teams and graceful degradation under low ambient discipline; the perennial discipline requires the opposite — sustained taste at every keystroke, an internalized language, individual judgment everywhere — and it does not survive replacement-friendly economics. These are the conditions Ousterhout described honestly: accept the team you have, design defensively around median taste, do not enforce what the substrate will defeat. For most software he was right, and he stays right.

Agents are a different substrate, and on it the calculus inverts twice. Doctrine inverts: with humans, methodology is downstream of practice — a retrospective articulation read by few and lived by fewer, dying in the gap between what we say we do and what we do; with agents, doctrine is upstream — loaded at priming, it *is* how the workforce behaves, and writing it stops being description and becomes specification. Transmission inverts with it: a refinement that took a generation of mentorship to spread now propagates to every subsequent agent invocation — firmware, not folklore. Ousterhout is not refuted; he is bypassed, for the one class of work whose binding constraint his substrate could never serve. And the cost arrives in the same motion: with humans, distributed taste was a backstop against bad doctrine; with agents, whatever is written becomes how the workforce behaves, mistakes included. The doctrine author is load-bearing — which is why this framework tests its prose like code, and records where its own doctrine failed.

## The re-derivation, worked

When the integrator is an agent, integration toil decouples from integration frequency. Fowler's curve was steep because reconciliation spent human attention; when the grinding part of reconciliation is cheap, the curve flattens without vanishing. Batch size stops being a moral and becomes a dial, with continuous integration as the limit case — the right setting sometimes, the mandated setting never. Working the dial forces a distinction the dogma never needed: Component-green, what a repository's own checks assert continuously on every change, and integration-green, what an episodic, judged integration event asserts about the assembled whole. And discipline survives the loosened cadence: any fix made mid-integration is a spike — exercised, never shipped — that flows back as a reviewed, merged PR. The [vocabulary's Stochastic integration entry](../doctrine/vocabulary.md#stochastic-integration) carries the law; this essay carries the argument.

Run the same derivation for branches. Long-lived branches were abandoned because humans could not function inside the divergence window: working memory too small to hold both worlds, reconciliation too slow to be safe — the morale damage in the merge-conflict studies is exactly that reconciliation's cost. An agent whose curated context window holds the entire divergence whole *can* function there. So the abandoned practice returns as the [Track](../doctrine/vocabulary.md#track): a divergence held jointly, invisible until published, governed by the publication gate — meaning reaches main before anything outside the Track builds against it. The gate is the discipline humans lacked — why they were right to abandon the practice, and why it can return.

## The premise, against its strongest counter

All of this rests on one premise — the agent's grind is cheap — and it must be stated precisely, because the strongest evidence looks hostile. A randomized controlled trial found experienced open-source developers 19% *slower* with AI assistance, and a meta-analysis found human–AI combinations underperforming the better constituent on average (Vaccaro et al., Nature Human Behaviour, 2024). If the premise were "AI makes engineering faster," it would now be dead.

The premise is narrower. The agent's unit of mechanical labor — a reconciliation attempted, a build ground green, a divergence re-derived — is cheap. Whether the *system* profits depends entirely on the coordination layer: how work is divided, what each party holds, where meaning survives the hand-offs. The hostile findings locate the failure at precisely that layer — combinations underperform even when a constituent is strong. That is not evidence against building on cheap agent grind; it is evidence that the coordination layer is where the engineering problem now lives — the layer this framework exists to engineer.

## The test

The question to put to any inherited practice is not "is this best practice" — that phrase is the sediment of someone else's argument — but "whose cost structure was this answering, and is that the cost structure at hand?" Then run the derivation and accept where it lands: the familiar answer, now held on purpose rather than by inertia; an abandoned answer, now armed with the discipline whose absence doomed it; or something with no precedent. Run honestly, it defends tests and review as firmly as it demotes integration cadence from rule to dial. That symmetry is the method; anything less is contrarianism wearing its clothes.
