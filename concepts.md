# Concepts

[The point](./the-point.md) argues why this framework exists and for what class of work; [prerequisites](./prerequisites.md) argues whether you should adopt it. This document is the one in between: the vocabulary the other two lean on, built up in order so that the terms stop being a glossary and become a system you can hold in your head. [Workflow](./workflow.md) is the same world set in motion: the lifecycle as a sequence of operator moves. Here the concern is structure rather than sequence: what each piece *is*, why it is shaped the way it is, and how it pulls on the pieces around it.

This is exposition, not reference. Every term has a precise definition, given once, in [`vocabulary.md`](doctrine/vocabulary.md): the single source of truth both the human and the agent trees draw on. This guide will not restate those definitions; it will introduce the terms in the order that makes them cohere, and link each to its entry the first time it appears. Read the two together: this for the connective tissue, the vocabulary for the contract.

## The show and the act

[**Vaudeville**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#vaudeville) was the variety stage: a parade of ephemeral acts, a juggler, a singer, a comedian, each gone the moment it stepped offstage, all held coherent under one show by a single direction. The framework takes the metaphor literally, because the claim the metaphor makes is the claim the framework makes: the value is not in any one act, however brilliant, but in the discipline that keeps a procession of them from drifting into a different show.

The act is the [**Bob**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#bob): a fresh Claude Code session spawned into its own worktree, handed one assignment, run until the work is delivered, then gone. A Bob's session does not survive a reboot, and it carries no memory of the sessions that worked the repository before it. The name nods to the Bobiverse novels, in which one mind is copied into many and each copy diverges from the moment it wakes. Every Bob forks from the same primed starting point and then lives its own short, particular life.

The ephemerality is the design, not a limitation being managed. Because a Bob remembers nothing it was not given, everything in its head can be put there deliberately. Its starting context is *compiled* rather than *accumulated*. That single property is the pivot the rest of the system turns on. The conviction underneath it is worth stating plainly, because every other piece depends on it holding: a Bob is frontier-model capable, and treating it as less (hedging it, pre-chewing its work, fencing it in) produces worse output, not safer output.

## Compiled, not accumulated

If a Bob remembers nothing it was not given, the question is what to give it. The framework's answer comes in two parts.

The [**Foundation**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#foundation) is the primed session every Bob in a given repository forks from. Priming replays a controlled sequence of source material as conversation history (the framework's [**Doctrine**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#doctrine) first, then the project's cross-cutting conventions, then the repository's own spec and vocabulary) so that when a Bob wakes, it already holds the discipline, the conventions, and the domain as lived context rather than as documents it must reason about cold. One Foundation per repository, rebuilt when its inputs change.

The [**Brief**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#brief) is the first message the freshly-spawned Bob reads: its assignment, and the framing for the kind of work that assignment expects. The Foundation is everything a Bob inherits; the Brief is the one thing it is for. Together they are the entire contents of its head at the moment it starts. Every item in that head was either primed deliberately or composed deliberately.

This is the framework's answer to the question other systems answer with "memory," and the difference is the whole argument. Memory asks what can be *recalled*; this model asks what is *admissible*. A fact can be true, relevant somewhere, and still poisonous in the current frame, because it drags in the wrong ontology or a stale authority. Ambient memory is treated here as contamination. The Foundation is the clean room each act steps into. This is why a Bob can be trusted with broad autonomy: not despite knowing little, but because everything it knows was chosen.

## The unit of work: the Assignment and its four kinds

What a Bob is handed is an [**Assignment**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#assignment), and almost everything about how the work goes is decided by which of four kinds it is. Two questions, asked in order, tell them apart. The operator answers them when filing the work, before any session exists. Take one piece of work, the audit log, and turn it through both.

First: **is the goal itself still open to argue?** If what we are even trying to do is the thing in contention, the assignment is a [**Premise**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#premise): *is event-sourcing the right model for the audit log at all?* The goal is the argument, and the Bob's job is to press it.

If the goal is settled, a second question follows: **is the way to it still open?** Ends fixed, means open, and the assignment is a [**Direction**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#direction): *move the audit log to event-sourcing; work out how.* The destination is chosen and rests on a single named assumption, its *hinge* (that event-sourcing is the right model), stated plainly so the Bob can attack it if the work proves it wrong; everything past that bet is the Bob's to find.

When both are settled, goal and steps alike, the assignment is a [**Command**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#command): *run the audit-log migration in these steps.* Marching orders the operator has worked out and hands off to be executed rather than pressed.

And when the operator delegates neither question (keeping the wheel and releasing intent turn by turn instead of apportioning it up front) the assignment is a [**Manual**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#manual): a primed Bob that reads, investigates, and raises questions but changes nothing until told. A Manual answers neither question, because the decision was never handed off.

Three of the four fall on one line. Premise, Direction, Command each settle more than the last (every kind fixing what the one before left open) so the kind is a record of how much its author has already decided. Manual is not the far end of that line but a step off it: the operator hands over nothing. The pairing the line skips (means settled while the ends stay open) names nothing real, because you cannot lay the road to a place you have not yet decided is worth reaching. That family of four is the Assignment. An agent may author a Premise or a Direction (proposing a goal, or a call with its hinge left exposed, is what a peer does) but never a Command or a Manual, whose authority is the operator's to confer.

Naming the family is what lets you see the seam a ticket hides. The human world hands work down as one undifferentiated unit (a *ticket*) and trusts the reader to infer its mode from shared context and a conversation in the hall. A Bob has neither; it knows only what its first turn hands it, so the mode has to be made explicit. The four kinds are exactly the modalities a ticket runs together, told apart by where discretion lies. The sharpest symptom of the conflation is a Premise that has sprouted a list of ordered steps. That is not a more careful Premise; it is a Premise wearing a Command's clothes: settled authority smuggled into the one kind whose authority has to stay open to its reader.

The Premise is the framework's most deliberate departure from industry habit, and the kind the rest of this canon leans on, so it earns a closer look. Because its goal stays open, a Premise is written as a humble snapshot (what one fallible voice understood at one moment) to be cross-checked against the live code, the authoritative tiebreaker when the two disagree, and pressed rather than obeyed. Author and reader are in different conversations, often different weeks, and the conversation that produced it is gone by the time it is read. Its reader is a peer being briefed, not a worker receiving instructions, and the measure follows: a wrong framing that surfaced disagreement early has done its job, while a Premise the reader silently followed into wrong work has failed even if its framing was technically defensible.

This is why [**acceptance criteria**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#acceptance-criteria) (the ticket-writer's checklist for when work is done) are banned from the Premise. Between two humans with shared context they are harmless; here the reader is a model trained on the world's project-management artifacts, and presented with a list shaped like acceptance criteria it treats the list as the contract and optimizes for satisfying it, exactly when the framework needs it cross-examining the framing instead. The reflex lives downstream of training, where no amount of hedging reaches it, so the correction must be made at the only end where it can: the author does not write them. What replaces them is situational awareness (the relevant context, the open questions, the author's guesses marked clearly as fallible) and, once a promise has genuinely settled, a behavioral test with teeth, which is what a contract looks like when it is real. The ban is the Premise's own, not a rule against ordered steps as such: a Command is rightly made of them, because its authority is already settled.

## How a kind is routed

The kind fixes how much the operator has already decided; a finer classification decides the shape of the conversation that carries the work out. That is the [**Route**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#route). The thing to hold onto is that it is not a single dial the genus turns the same way for every kind. It is a property each kind constrains: the kind comes first, and the Route is what that kind still leaves open to choose.

There are four route values, and they are the framework's three modes of intervention made concrete. A process that can be specified exhaustively belongs in deterministic tooling, not a conversation at all. Judgment within bounded scope belongs in a bounded conversation with a beginning, a middle, and an end. Genuinely open judgment (cross-cutting, with no fixed deliverable a lifecycle could anticipate) belongs in an open conversation that follows the work wherever it goes. The values: **direct**, mechanical and CI-gated, realized with no check-in; **check-in**, a bounded decision point where human judgment is wanted before the work merges; **plan**, whose deliverable is a set of new Premises and Directions rather than code; and **explore**, the open conversation, which exists because open-ended human-agent dialogue is first-class work, not a scaffold failure to be engineered away.

Which of the four a kind may take is fixed by the kind. A Premise takes check-in, plan, or explore, never direct, because a goal settled enough to realize with no check-in is no longer a proposition but a Command. A Direction takes check-in only: its means are open, so the work wants a conversation, but its ends are settled, so the conversation does not reopen the goal. A Command takes check-in or direct: direct when the operator wants it run to completion, check-in when the *what* should be cleared before the work merges. A Manual takes no route at all, because a route classifies a conversation planned before the work begins, and a Manual's intent arrives turn by turn instead. [Workflow](./workflow.md) shows what these look like as they run.

The Route is fixed once, before the work begins, and it classifies *between* sessions: the authoring conversation decides what shape the working conversation should take. The same classifying instinct returns one level down, *inside* a session. Once a Bob is underway it still must decide how to realize the work in hand, and that decision has its own small taxonomy; the context that runs the sessions calls this session-level counterpart **Procedure**. The pairing is deliberate. Route is the coordination-level classification: fixed before any session exists, and shared across every context the framework serves. Procedure is the session-level one: chosen inside a running session, and owned by the context whose machinery runs it. They are the same instinct at two scales, which is why the framework keeps them a matched pair rather than folding them into one word: deciding what kind of conversation to open is a Route question; deciding how to carry the work once it is open is a Procedure question.

The middle tier of that sorting (bounded judgment in a bounded conversation) is where a [**Skill**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#skill) lives. A Skill is a documented procedure an agent invokes by name (`/file`, `/realize`, `/closeout`), scaffolding that gives a procedure a shape without reducing it to deterministic code; the agent's judgment fills the shape in. Skills are explicitly waypoints toward rote delegation, not destinations. When a piece of what a Skill exhorts stabilizes into something fully mechanical, it is compiled out of the Skill and into a verb of the `vv` command-line tool, and the agent stops reasoning about what has become machinery. A mature Skill hollows out over time (judgment evaporating into tooling) and that hollowing is the system getting healthier, not poorer: less of the essential structure left depending on discretion.

## Jurisdictions of meaning

A Bob does its work somewhere, and where it works is not an incidental fact. A [**Context**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#context) is what domain-driven design calls a bounded context, given sharper teeth here as a semantic jurisdiction: it owns its internal vocabulary, types, and architecture, and agents in one do not reach into another's internals. The reason runs deeper than tidiness. A language model is a pattern-completion engine, and words drag worlds behind them; let one context's vocabulary leak into another and the model quietly blends their ontologies, building the nearest familiar thing instead of the actual system. The boundary is what keeps each context's meaning crisp enough to reason about. It is permeable on purpose (an agent may look into a neighboring context for evidence about how the pieces interact) under one compact rule: neighbor context is evidence, contracts are authority. And the boundary is facilitation, not a fence: a clearly named remit inside which the agent is free, in a system designed so it has no need to wander out.

What the framework actually manages is not quite the Context. Consider the doctrine you are reading: it is worked exactly as a Context is, spawned into and primed, assigned its own work, yet it adjudicates no domain of its own. It runs no court of its own meanings; it is material every Context draws on. To the machinery the doctrine and a Context are the same sort of thing, and the name for what they share is the [**Component**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#component), the unit Vaudeville manages. A Component is realized as a git repository, but the repository is only where it is kept, never what it is. One question sorts every Component, and it turns on what a Component does, not what it holds: does it adjudicate a domain of its own? A Context does, and that adjudication is the whole of what makes it a Context. A [**Resource**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#resource) does not: it holds material a Context draws on (configuration, assets, glue, the doctrine itself) without holding a jurisdiction. The cut is by jurisdiction and not by content, which is why the doctrine is a Resource even though it is dense with domain material: carrying a domain is not the same as adjudicating one. And it is why naming the unit for its repository would be the telling mistake, binding a jurisdiction of meaning to the storage that merely holds it.

## Whose show it is

The Components a Bob works in are never the whole of anything; each is one part of a larger effort. That whole is a [**Project**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#project): an ensemble of related Components that together make one cohesive engineering effort. Most Projects run no framework at all. The few that adopt Vaudeville are the ones the framework serves, and the framework refuses to assume there is only ever one of them. A [**Tenant**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#tenant) is the role a Project plays toward the machinery: a Project becomes a Tenant the way a person becomes an author, by standing in a relation to something, not by rising to a rank above it. The Tenant is the one effort, of the many that could, that a given install actually serves, with its own configuration and its own Bobs; equivalently, the user, team, or organization running Vaudeville on its own host. That refusal to assume a single Tenant has a concrete consequence: Doctrine is split into a universal layer every Tenant receives unchanged and a per-Tenant layer each adds for itself, and the split is enforced now, while it is cheap, rather than retrofitted later. This is where the two senses of the name come apart. Vaudeville *the framework* is the methodology and tooling a Tenant installs; Vaudeville *the project* is the development effort that builds the framework, itself a Project, and the Tenant running the next version of itself on the most recent release of itself. [The show runs on itself](walkthrough.md), but the framework is built so it would run for someone else.

That universal layer is the [**Doctrine**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#doctrine): the framing documents an agent reads as priming before it does any work, and the single source of truth for what it should know before its first turn. Doctrine comes in two trees for two readers. The operative tree is compressed hard, because it sits at the most upstream point of every chain of hand-offs. Every token trimmed from it without loss is saved on every turn of every Bob, for as long as the framework runs. The human-facing tree, the one you are reading now, is under no such pressure; it is where the convictions get to breathe. The two are not the same text twice (they carry different things for different readers) and what they share is the one vocabulary both draw on. The writing discipline for Doctrine is unusual enough to name: it is written for a reader at least as capable as its author, often a newer and stronger model arriving between when a passage is written and when it is read, so it explains nothing the next agent could work out alone, and its most valuable edit is frequently a deletion.

## The slack that makes it hold

One concept underlies every boundary the framework draws. A [**Tolerance**](doctrine/vocabulary.md#tolerance) is the slack at an interface that lets imperfect parts fit together. Here it is slack designed in on purpose, not an imperfection awaiting cleanup. The term is borrowed from mechanical engineering, where a part machined to impossible precision does not make a better assembly; it makes one that binds. Strict control of agent behavior narrows the mating surface (every interior decision legislated, judgment replaced with rule-following) and that micromanagement produces fragility that reads, from a distance, as rigor. The alternative is to draw well-defined remits (Contexts, scoped Skills, narrow interfaces) inside which the agent is free to act, in a system arranged so the correct path is also the natural one. Tolerance is the name for what those remits are sized to leave room for, and it applies at every interface the framework has: between software modules, between human and agent, between agent and scaffold.

## How the pieces connect

Read in order, the terms assemble into one motion. An ephemeral, capable act (the Bob) is given a compiled starting context (its Foundation and Brief) and an Assignment: most often a Premise, the humble proposition that is never a ticket and never a list of acceptance criteria; a Direction or a Command as the operator has settled more before handing off; and, off that line entirely, a Manual, where the operator keeps the wheel and releases intent live. That work is classified first by kind and then, where the kind admits one, by Route, into the shape of conversation it deserves, and carried out with the help of Skills that hollow into tooling as they mature. All of it happens inside Components (a Context where the work adjudicates a domain, a Resource where it does not) that compose into a Project, the one a Tenant runs on its own host, primed from a shared Doctrine, and held together not by tight control but by Tolerance: slack deliberately left where imperfect parts have to meet.

That is the system as a set of parts. To watch the same parts move (a Premise filed, a Bob spawned, a check-in, a merge, a closeout, and the next work unblocked) read [workflow](./workflow.md). For the precise definition of any term above, read its entry in [`vocabulary.md`](doctrine/vocabulary.md).
